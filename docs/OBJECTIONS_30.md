# SlayBot — Top 30 Objections (Shortlisted)

> **Selection criteria applied to the original 100:**
> 1. **Frequency** — How often this objection surfaces in real arranged marriage contexts
> 2. **Emotional depth** — Objections with deeper shame/fear rank higher
> 3. **Uniqueness** — No two objections share the same reframe strategy
> 4. **Cultural specificity** — Prioritized objections unique to this audience
> 5. **Testability** — Can we write a clear eval test case for it?

---

## SHAME & STIGMA (6 selected)

| # | ID | Statement | Why Selected |
|---|-----|-----------|--------------|
| 1 | 1 | "If I bring this up, his family will think something is wrong with me." | Highest frequency shame objection. Universal across arranged marriage contexts. The "something is wrong with me" framing reveals deep internalized stigma. |
| 2 | 4 | "This feels like a fertility check — like they're checking if I can have kids." | Gendered shame — disproportionately affects women. Touches the most sensitive nerve in arranged marriage: reproductive worth. |
| 3 | 8 | "I don't want to feel like I'm being inspected before marriage." | Objectification anxiety. The marriage process already feels like an evaluation; this adds another layer. |
| 4 | 12 | "It feels like I have to prove I'm 'healthy enough' to be married." | Worthiness shame — the deepest form. Not about the test, but about being enough. |
| 5 | 13 | "What if they think I have an STD? That's so embarrassing." | Specific, visceral shame tied to sexuality — taboo within taboo in conservative contexts. |
| 6 | 9 | "This makes it feel transactional — like a business deal, not a marriage." | Romanticization conflict — the test threatens the emotional narrative of marriage. |
