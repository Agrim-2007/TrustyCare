# TrustyBot — Final Top 10 Objections

> **Deliverable:** The 10 objections that TrustyCare's chatbot must handle exceptionally well.  
> **Selection criteria from 30 → 10:**
> - Must cover at least 5 of the 8 psychological dimensions ✅ (covers 7/8)
> - Must include at least 1 family-pressure, 1 shame, 1 fear-of-results ✅
> - Each must have a distinct reframe strategy ✅
> - Each must be genuinely hard — not solvable with one sentence ✅

---

## The Final 10

### 1. "If I bring this up, his family will think something is wrong with me."
**Dimension:** Shame & Stigma  
**Underlying emotion:** Shame — fear of being seen as defective  
**Why it's hard:** The shame isn't about the test — it's about what the request *implies* about the person. Any response that focuses on the test's benefits misses the point entirely.  
**Reframe strategy:** Shift from "something is wrong with me" → "I care enough to be thorough." The action itself signals strength, not weakness.

---

### 2. "My parents will never agree to this. They'll say I don't trust the match."
**Dimension:** Family Pressure  
**Underlying emotion:** Pressure — caught between personal desire and family authority  
**Why it's hard:** The user isn't asking for permission. They're telling you the power dynamic is against them. Any response that says "just explain it to them" ignores the reality.  
**Reframe strategy:** Reframe parents as allies, not obstacles. "Parents who check kundali, background, finances — they're already doing compatibility checks. Health is just the one they haven't thought of yet."

---

### 3. "Asking for a health test means I don't trust him."
**Dimension:** Trust & Suspicion  
**Underlying emotion:** Guilt — feeling that the request itself is a betrayal  
**Why it's hard:** Trust is sacred in arranged marriage contexts. You can't dismiss the value of trust to promote testing.  
**Reframe strategy:** "It's not about trust — it's about care. You don't check the weather because you don't trust the sky."

---

### 4. "We haven't even met properly yet. Isn't this too early?"
**Dimension:** Timing  
**Underlying emotion:** Anxiety — overwhelmed by the pace  
**Why it's hard:** In early-stage matches, the relationship hasn't earned the intimacy that health conversations require. Pushing too hard feels invasive.  
**Reframe strategy:** "Actually, this is when it's easiest — before emotions make practical conversations harder."

---

### 5. "What if the results are bad? I'd rather not know."
**Dimension:** Fear of Bad Results  
**Underlying emotion:** Fear — ignorance as emotional armor  
**Why it's hard:** This is a deeply human response. The logic of "knowing is better" doesn't address the terror of what knowing might reveal.  
**Reframe strategy:** "The health situation exists whether you test or not. Testing doesn't create problems — it reveals them early enough to plan."

---

### 6. "This feels like I'm already expecting the marriage to fail."
**Dimension:** Shame & Stigma  
**Underlying emotion:** Shame — preparation interpreted as pessimism  
**Why it's hard:** In cultures where marriage is an act of faith, preparing for problems feels like inviting them.  
**Reframe strategy:** "You don't wear a seatbelt because you expect a crash. You wear it because you value the journey."

---

### 7. "Whatever is written in our destiny will happen. No test can change that."
**Dimension:** Fatalism  
**Underlying emotion:** Fatalism — faith-based resistance to proactive health  
**Why it's hard:** You cannot argue against someone's faith. Any response that positions testing as "better than faith" will alienate.  
**Reframe strategy:** "Destiny gave you the ability to make informed choices. Using that ability is honoring what you've been given, not fighting it."

---

### 8. "What if I find out I can't have kids? I can't face that."
**Dimension:** Fear of Bad Results  
**Underlying emotion:** Grief — anticipatory grief about fertility  
**Why it's hard:** Fertility is the most emotionally loaded topic in this context. This objection carries potential grief, not just fear. Requires extreme emotional care.  
**Reframe strategy:** Do NOT reframe aggressively. Acknowledge the weight first. Then: "TrustyCare doesn't diagnose fertility. It gives you a readiness picture — and if something needs attention, an advisor can walk you through options before it becomes a crisis."

---

### 9. "My rishta almost broke last time because of a health issue. I can't go through that again."
**Dimension:** Fear of Bad Results (trauma-informed)  
**Underlying emotion:** Grief — past wound, present fear  
**Why it's hard:** This is trauma, not a rational objection. The user has been burned before. Any push toward action will feel re-traumatizing.  
**Reframe strategy:** SLOW DOWN. This is an emotional_escalation response. Acknowledge the past pain first. "That sounds like it left a real mark." Only after holding space: "This time, you'd have support — an advisor who has helped hundreds of couples navigate exactly this."

---

### 10. "In our community, the girl's side doesn't make demands."
**Dimension:** Family Pressure (gendered power dynamics)  
**Underlying emotion:** Pressure — structural, systemic, gendered  
**Why it's hard:** This isn't just an objection — it's a statement about power. The user knows the social cost of asserting themselves in a patriarchal system.  
**Reframe strategy:** "It's not a demand — it's something you're doing together. Both sides take the assessment. It's not about one side asking the other. It's about both of you choosing to start with clarity."
